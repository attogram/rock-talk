## Description
Fair enough. Reviewing it as a protocol definition paper, not an empirical study.

**What works**

The framing is honest — this is a specification document with a proposed validation framework, and it reads that way. The three-hypothesis structure in Section 7 is well-formed: each has a clear claim, experimental design, and analysis strategy. The paired t-test, Needle-in-a-Haystack adaptation, and Cascade Failure Rate metric are all appropriate choices for their respective hypotheses. Someone could actually run these.

The elasticity spectrum (Section 5.1) is the strongest conceptual contribution. The three-tier table is clear and the Givón/Levinson grounding is legitimate. This is what separates the protocol from a joke about cavemen.

The TIR and SDI formalizations are simple but useful — gives future researchers a shared vocabulary.

**Areas to strengthen**

The motivating incident is charming but the auto-ethnographic citation will distract readers. Consider reframing it as an "Observed Incident" with a footnote rather than a formal citation.

Section 4 (archetypes) does real work distinguishing Rock Talk from superficially similar patterns, but the Ooga Booga Fallacy and Unfrozen Caveman Lawyer deserve more development — especially the adversarial cloaking risk, which is a genuinely novel alignment observation buried at the end of a pop culture section.

The bidirectional vs. one-sided protocol distinction (Section 10) is underexplored relative to its importance. This seems like a key differentiator for v2.

**Overall:** Solid v1 foundation. The bones are good.
