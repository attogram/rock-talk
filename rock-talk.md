| **Abstract** | **Abstract** |
| This paper introduces Rock Talk 1.0, a communication protocol designed to maximize information density by systematically<br>removing linguistic "packaging"—the social, syntactical, and conversational scaffolding that characterizes natural<br>language.<br><br>Drawing on Shannon’s (1948) mathematical theory of communication, we hypothesize that by minimizing linguistic<br>entropy and maximizing the signal-to-noise ratio, Rock Talk improves alignment and efficiency in Human-to-Large<br>Language Model (LLM) interactions and Agentic Coordination.<br><br>Preliminary observations suggest that Rock Talk significantly reduces token consumption and mitigates "attention<br>drift," providing a robust framework for high-stakes technical environments. | Rock Talk 1.0.<br>Maximize info.<br>Remove noise.<br>Better Human-LLM work.<br>Better Agentic Coordination.<br>High signal.<br>Shannon 1948.<br>Hypothesis: Less tokens, better alignment.<br>Stop model drift. |
| **1. Introduction** | **1. Introduction** |
| Modern human communication is saturated with "packaging"—hedging, politeness markers, and redundant structural<br>cues.<br><br>Malinowski (1923) characterized this as "phatic communion," language used primarily to establish social atmosphere<br>rather than to convey meaning.<br><br>While these serve social cohesion, we observe they may introduce significant entropy in technical and computational<br>contexts. | Human talk has noise.<br>Polite words, extra grammar.<br>Good for friends.<br>Bad for work.<br>Phatic noise (Malinowski 1923).<br>Social signals, not data.<br>Entropy high. |
| We propose "Rock Talk," a protocol that prioritizes the "payload" of a message over its social delivery.<br><br>Despite its superficial resemblance to primitive speech patterns, we hypothesize that Rock Talk is a sophisticated<br>method of information compression and intent-loading, echoing the "Principle of Least Effort" found in natural<br>language evolution (Zipf, 1949). | Proposal: Rock Talk.<br>Payload first.<br>Delivery second.<br>Looks simple.<br>Is compression.<br>Least effort (Zipf 1949).<br>Intent-loading. |

| **2. Theoretical Framework** | **2. Theoretical Framework** |
| Information theory suggests that the efficiency of a channel is determined by its signal-to-noise ratio<br>(Shannon, 1948).<br><br>Rock Talk functions as a "lossless compression" for intent, minimizing the articulatory and computational cost of<br>transmission. | Info theory: Signal vs Noise (Shannon 1948).<br>Rock Talk: Lossless compression.<br>Keep core data.<br>Drop wrapper.<br>Low cost. |
| The protocol aligns with Grice’s (1975) Cooperative Principle, specifically the Maxim of Manner: "be brief (avoid<br>unnecessary prolixity)."<br><br>Furthermore, it leverages Clark’s (1996) concept of "common ground," assuming that shared technical context<br>permits the removal of redundant scaffolding without semantic degradation.<br><br>Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson, 1986) further supports this, suggesting that optimal communication achieves<br>maximum cognitive effect with minimum processing effort. | Grice 1975: Be brief.<br>No extra words.<br>Use common ground (Clark 1996).<br>Relevance Theory (Sperber & Wilson 1986): Max effect, min work. |
| **3. Professional High-Signal Archetypes** | **3. Professional High-Signal Archetypes** |
| Rock Talk finds its most robust real-world precedents in mission-critical domains where latency and ambiguity are<br>life-threatening.<br><br>Air Traffic Control (ATC) utilizes a standardized "Pilot/Controller Glossary" (FAA, 2026) to ensure "readability,<br>and a minimum of words." [FAA Glossary](https://www.faa.gov/air_traffic/publications/media/PCG_Bsc_w_Chg_1_and_2_dtd_1-22-26.pdf)<br><br>Similarly, Multi-Service Brevity Codes (ALSSA, 2025) provide standardized, single-word "payloads" for complex<br>tactical situations. [ALSSA Brevity](https://www.alssa.mil/mttps/brevity/) | Real work: ATC, Military.<br>High stakes, no lag.<br>FAA (2026): Clear, fast, short.<br>ALSSA (2025): Brevity codes.<br>One word = huge data. |
| Historical "Telegraphese" or "Telegram Style" (Standage, 1998) demonstrates an economic driver for information<br>density.<br><br>By charging per word, telegraph companies incentivized the systematic removal of articles and non-essential syntax<br>(Hochfelder, 2012), creating a proto-Rock Talk for the 19th-century "Victorian Internet." | History: Telegrams.<br>Pay per word.<br>Remove extra grammar (Hochfelder 2012).<br>Victorian Internet (Standage 1998).<br>Money drives signal. |
